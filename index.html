Data Mesh Architecture

Data Mesh from an Engineering Perspective

Data Mesh Architecture is a decentralised architecture that enables domain teams to perform cross-domain data analysis on their own.

Why?

Data Mesh enables data-driven decisions within our domain.

Central data teams failed to scale, giving meaningful insights from data due to limited domain knowledge.

Many organizations have already made a transformation towards domain-oriented teams and systems. As a result, domain teams have a deep understanding of their processes and data.

Data Mesh adopts the idea of domain-oriented decentralization for analytical data. A Data Mesh Architecture gives domain teams the capabilities to analyze data on their own and to provide curated data to other domains.

Domain Team's Journey

Each of your domain teams has to go on a journey to become a contributing part of your data mesh. Each team can start their journey whenever they are ready and in their own pace. The benefits arise already along the journey. Teams will quickly gain from first data-driven decisions, starting an avalanche to use more and better data for even deeper insights. The data mesh forms itself with each team that shares their data as products, enabling data-driven innovation.

To make this journey successful, the team needs three things: a clear data mesh vision from top management to get everybody moving in the same direction, a supportive environment including an easy to use self-serve data platform to get the engineering team on a learning path towards data analytics, and trust to walk the journey in their own way and pace.

So let's start your journey!

Level 0 No Data Analytics

Your autonomous team is responsible for a domain and builds and operates self-contained systems including the neccessary infrastructure. These operational systems now generate domain data.

It was quite an effort to reach this level and you were highly focussed on delivery excellence. So, you didn't have capacity to do data analytics during that transformation as well. Others, like a central data team, however, may export your production database to a central data lake for data analysis and management reports. You might feel uncomfortable that others had direct access to your production databases, violating the principle of information hiding that so worked so hard to achieve in your microservices. Some management reports may include insights on your domain, but you don't really understand how the numbers were calculated and nobody can validate if these were correct.

Level 1 Operational Database Queries

Your team starts to send queries to your operational database. You probably have to investigate a production incident and need to analyze how many customers are affected. You also might query your database to answer someone's questions regarding your data, or you do some explorative analytics to get a deeper understanding of your system's behaviour.

This increases load on your production database, so you might be tempted to change the production database to better support your analytical queries, like creating additional indices. And this feels very wrong. Although you might offload the additional load to a secondary database node, it doesn't solve the performance issues.

In summary, the valuable insights of your analytical efforts are shaded by performance optimization trade-offs.

Level 2 Analyze Own Data

You have heard that some data platform team is providing a new data platform for every team to use, called _Data Mesh_. You give it a try. You build an analytical data model from your Kafka messages while keeping all operational databases untouched. This allows you to analyze data covering all your own systems performantly.

You learn how to structure, preprocess, clean, analyze, and visualize analytical data—that's a lot to learn even though most is SQL which most engineers of your team are already familiar with. Fortunately, there are a number of templates for typical use cases that you can copy and adopt for your needs. An enablement team helps you, how to build more complex queries.

Because questions regarding your own data can now be answered quickly, you now enter the cycle of making data-driven decisions: define hypotheses and verify with data.

Level 3 Analyze Cross-domain Data

Analyzing own domain data is a great start, but combining it with data from other domains is where the magic begins. It allows to get a comprehensive view despite the decentralization of data. Examples are the correlation of domain events with the conversion or building up machine learning models. This requires that other teams share their data in a way that your team can discover, access, and use it. This is when the mesh begins to form itself.

When a team becomes a consuming member of the data mesh, it starts to gain interest in the interoperability and governance of the data mesh. Ideally, the team will send a representative to the data mesh governance body.

In case you are the first team, you may have to skip this step for now and move on to level 4 and be the first to provide data for others.

Level 4 Publish Data as a Product

Based on other teams need, you share your data with others as products. Instead of just being a consumer of data products, the domain teams becomes a producer of data products. You generate value for other teams. But at the same time, it increases your responsibility and operational duties.

Data Team's Journey

Data Mesh is primarily an organizational construct and relies on the principles of team topologies. It shifts the responsibilities for data towards domain teams which are supported by a data platform team and a data enablement team. Representatives of all teams come together in a federated governance guild to define the common standards.

Today, in many organizations a central data team is responsible for wide-ranging analytical tasks, from data-engineering to managing data infrastructure to creating C-level reports. Such a central data team suffers from cognitive overload, including domain, technical, and methodical knowledge. Data Mesh mitigates this. It offers new perspectives for members of the central data team as their analytical and data engineering skills remain highly necessary. For example, they are a perfect fit to establish the data platform for people that prefer to work on the infrastructure. Some of them can form a data enablement team to act as internal consultants, helping domain teams on their journey. Despite their new roles, they are also destined to contribute in the Data Mesh federated governance guild, which is responsible to agree on the interoperability of data products and enforcing regulations.
